Benifits and Difficulties of Buddhism

4 Noble Truths

Dukkha: all life is suffering, pain, and misery.

Tanha: this suffering has a cause.

Nirodha: the termination of selfish craving can be achieved.

Magga: the way to overcome this misery is through the Eightfold Path.

8 Fold Path

1. Right Knowledge is knowledge of what life is all about; knowledge of the Four Noble Truths is basic to any further growth as a Buddhist.

2. Right Aspiration means a clear devotion to being on the Path toward Enlightenment

3. Right Speech involves both clarity of what is said (taking care to say just what is meant) and speaking kindly and without bitterness.

4. Right Behavior involves reflecting on the behavior of one, and the reasons for it. It also involves five basic laws of behavior for Buddhists: not to kill, steal, lie, drink intoxicants, or commit sexual offenses.

5. Right Livelihood involves choosing an occupation that keeps an individual on the Path; that is, a path that promotes life and well-being, rather than the accumulation of a lot of money.

6. Right Effort means training the will and curbing selfish passions and wants. It also means placing oneself along the Path toward Enlightenment.

7. Right Mindfulness implies continuing self-examination and awareness. The Dhammapada, a basic Buddhist text, begins: All we are is the result of what we have thought.

8. Right Concentration is the final goal, to be absorbed into a state of Nirvana

Who is Buddha?

Around the same time Confucius was teaching Chinese people how to lead the good life, a prince called Siddhartha Gautama, or Gotama, became famous for his holiness and love of all creatures. He was called the Enlightened One (Buddha) a title used by many groups in ancient India but associated now mostly with Gautama. Many persons believed in the teachings of Buddha while he lived. After his death, shrines called stupas were built in his honor, and his religion spread through a great part of Asia. By the early 21st century some 380 million people professed the Buddhist faith.

Why are we still talking about him 2500 years later?

Buddha is still talked about today because lots of people in Asia and more importantly people with power believed in what he preached and when Buddha died they kept the faith going with the help of the order of monks he created.

Main Types of Buddhism

The Lower Vehicle
AKA the way of the elders. Hinayana is the more conservative interpreters of the Buddhist teachings. Another name is Theravada Buddhism (this name is more respectful name for way of the elders). Hinayana is still the main religion of Sri Lanka, Myanmar, Thailand, Laos and Cambodia. Honor Buddha as a perfected master. They focus on the historical figure and stress the monastic life and the striving for wisdom.

The Greater Vehicle
AKA Mahayana. Mahayana is popular in Mongolia, Tibet, China, Japan, Korea, Vietnam and Nepal. It accepts a number more teachings and texts, but the main difference between the schools is how they see the life and teaching of Buddha. Mahayana Buddhists emphasize the heavenly nature of the Buddha and stress the importance of many different Buddhas.

Zen Buddhism
Is a derivative of the Mahayana school which has far more followers then Theravada schools. The most popular school of Buddhism in Japan is part of the Mahayana tradition.

Population of Buddhists

Approximately 7.5% of people in the world are Buddhists. They mainly live in Thailand and Cambodia with those to countries having about 95% of all Buddhists in their boundaries.

Letters to Buddha

Dear Buddha,
I am interested in becoming a Buddha and while researching, I struggled to grasp the concept of Buddhist pare. How do I pray and what to?
From
Peter H


Dear Peter,
Buddhist pare is done through meditation. Buddhist do not believe in a creator god. Meditation creates a happier life and allows us to properly reflect on what we need to. I suggest finding a Buddhist temple and participating with the services to understand how meditation works better and gives you needed practice. Good luck in your journey to Nirvana.
Buddha



Dear Buddha,
I am not a Buddhist but would like to attend a service at my local temple. Would this be allowed?
Thank you
Georgia M

Dear Georgia,
Most temples allow it. Though it is appropriate to call the temple ahead of time and inquire. The larger, more established temples often publish announcements in local newspapers to show the schedule of the next service. When attending you may participate in communal rituals, alternatively you can observe in silence from the back of the temple. I hope you enjoy the service when you attend.
Buddha
